§ 00 — Executive Summary
InvoiceIQ is a privacy-preserving AI early-warning system that predicts MSME cash flow
collapse 30–60 days before it happens — and automatically generates the interventions to prevent
it. ₹ 7.34 lakh crore is currently locked in delayed receivables across India’s 6.4 crore MSMEs.
Every existing solution (TReDS, Samadhaan, invoice financing) activates after the crisis has
begun, by which point the MSME has already taken informal credit at 36%+ interest. InvoiceIQ
shifts the entire paradigm from reactive collection to predictive survival.
What separates this system from any other product in the market is its cryptographic architecture:
the data the AI analyzes is never seen in plaintext, by anyone, including us. All buyer-risk
inference runs on ciphertext via Fully Homomorphic Encryption. Aggregate network signals are
protected by Differential Privacy. The informal cash economy — the reality of how MSMEs
actually operate — is bridged through a Telegram NLP layer with Maker-Checker controls.
This PRD is the consolidated, vulnerability-hardened final version of all prior plans. Every feature
has been preserved, merged, or upgraded with a documented replacement. Every cryptographic
claim has been scoped honestly. Every legal citation has been verified. Every demo failure mode
has been pre-mitigated.
§ 01 — The Problem: Six Structural Failures

The MSME cash flow crisis is not a single problem. It is six interlocking failures that no single
existing tool addresses:
Failure 01 — Reactivity. TReDS, Samadhaan, and invoice financing only activate after an
invoice is already overdue. By then the MSME has taken emergency credit at 36% APR. The
damage is structural.
Failure 02 — The Retaliation Trap. Section 15 of the MSMED Act gives MSMEs the right to
demand timely payment. Section 43B(h) of the Income Tax Act penalizes buyers who don’t pay
on time. But documented evidence shows large buyers cancel orders or move business to
unregistered suppliers when MSMEs assert these rights. The law gives the right; the market
punishes its use.
Failure 03 — Data Surrender. Every fintech analytics platform requires uploading complete
books to a third-party server. For an MSME, exposing buyer relationships, pricing, and revenue
concentration to a breachable cloud is existentially risky.
Failure 04 — Hidden Contractual Traps. Buyer agreements contain dispute-trigger clauses, set-
off rights, force-majeure loops, and invoicing-defect resets that legalize indefinite payment
delays. MSMEs sign without legal review.
Failure 05 — Decryption-in-Memory. Even encrypted-at-rest data must decrypt to RAM for AI
analysis. That moment is the attack surface for RAM scrapers, AI malware, and insider threats.
FHE eliminates this attack class entirely.
Failure 06 — Informal Economy Reality. Indian MSMEs operate in a mixed cash-digital world.
Petty cash, vendor payments, employee advances — none of it touches a digital ledger. Any
survival prediction built only on bank data is missing 30–50% of actual cash movement.
§ 02 — Goals & Success Metrics
The system targets five measurable outcomes for the hackathon demo and beyond:
Predict cash flow collapse 30–60 days in advance with 80% confidence intervals on the runway
figure. Process Telegram cash logs from informal employees in under 3 seconds end-to-end. Run
buyer reliability inference entirely on FHE-encrypted numeric features with zero plaintext server

exposure. Detect the four defined contract dark-pattern clause types with 100% recall on the
curated demo contract. Generate legally accurate intervention notices that correctly cite Section
15 of the MSMED Act 2006 and Section 43B(h) of the Income Tax Act 1961 with zero LLM-
introduced hallucinations.
§ 03 — Target Users
Primary: Indian MSME owners running businesses with 2–50 employees, operating in mixed
digital/cash workflows, lacking deep financial literacy, and facing systemic retaliation when
asserting legal rights against larger corporate buyers.
Secondary: MSME employees who handle petty cash and vendor payments on the owner’s
behalf, and need a frictionless way to log expenses through the channel they already use
(Telegram/WhatsApp).
The Reality Addressed: Users do not have clean digital ledgers. They cannot afford a CA on
retainer. They will not upload their books to a cloud they don’t trust. The product must work with
the informal economy, not against it.
§ 04 — Core Architecture: Six Integrated Layers
Layer 1 — Hybrid Cash-Digital Ledger
The Telegram Bot acts as the primary input channel for informal cash movements. An employee
texts “Paid 5k cash to Raju for transport” and a local Llama 3 instance (running through Ollama
in JSON-output mode) extracts a structured intent: {action: "expense", amount: 5000, vendor:
"Raju", category: "transport"}. A regex fallback parser catches any case where Llama returns
malformed JSON, ensuring the bot never silently fails. A vendor alias table maps colloquial
names to canonical vendor IDs to prevent duplicate entries.
Every transaction over ₹ 10,000 is automatically held in a separate pending_transactions table and
pings the MSME owner for one-tap approval before being committed to the main ledger. This
Maker-Checker control prevents both employee error and deliberate sabotage. Pending
transactions are explicitly excluded from runway math and clearly displayed on the dashboard as

“awaiting approval.” End-of-day cash reconciliation lets the owner enter till totals, and the system
computes unrecorded cash sales by reverse-balancing the day’s known movements.
The ₹ 10,000 threshold is the sensible default for the demo and is configurable post-launch per
MSME segment.
Layer 2 — Predictive Buyer Reliability Engine
For each buyer, the system computes payment behavior using methods that respect the actual data
distribution. Exponential Moving Average and Payment Velocity are used instead of simple
means, so the model snaps quickly to recent liquidity changes. Variance is captured via the
empirical 90th percentile of historical delays, not μ + 1.5σ — payment delay distributions are
heavily right-skewed (lognormal), and Gaussian assumptions systematically underestimate worst
cases. The Cold-Start Protocol maps unknown buyers to a sector-baseline lookup table (textile,
auto-ancillary, FMCG, etc.) derived from RBI MSME Pulse data, replacing arbitrary risk haircuts
with defensible numbers.
Meta’s Prophet is used only for aggregate cash inflow forecasting (where the time series has
enough data points to be reliable), not for per-buyer prediction. Per-buyer trend math uses EMA
and velocity, which are appropriate for the 6–12 invoice samples that real MSMEs have per buyer
per year.
The ML core is an XGBoost Buyer Reliability classifier compiled to FHE-compatible form via
Zama’s concrete-ml. Tree depth is capped at 4 and inputs are quantized to 8 bits to keep inference
latency in the 5–30 second range per query, which is then handled asynchronously.
Layer 3 — Cash Flow Collapse Predictor (Survival Runway)
The runway is a deterministic calculation, kept in plaintext on the client side because FHE adds
zero security value to local arithmetic on data the user already owns:
Risk-Adjusted Receivables = Σ(invoice_amount × (1 − overdue_probability)) for all open
invoices.
Survival Runway = (Digital Reserves + Cash Reserves + Risk-Adjusted Receivables) / Daily
Burn Rate.

Critically, the runway is never displayed as a single number. It always carries an 80% confidence
interval derived from Monte Carlo sampling over the buyer probability distributions. The
dashboard shows: “34 Days · 80% CI: 22–48 Days.” This single design choice converts the
product from “AI gimmick” to “scientifically defensible tool” and pre-empts the inevitable judge
question about uncertainty.
A Concentration Risk metric runs alongside the runway: “67% of your runway depends on Apex
Manufacturing.” This is a one-line Pandas calculation that surfaces the most actionable insight
any MSME owner can have.
Layer 4 — Contract Dark Pattern Detector
When the user uploads a buyer agreement PDF, pdfplumber extracts the text and a spaCy pattern
matcher with an expert-curated rule library identifies the four critical clause types: dispute-
triggered payment holds, set-off rights, force-majeure payment loops, and invoicing-defect
nullification clauses. This is rule-based by deliberate choice — fine-tuning BERT in 24 hours is
infeasible, dataset-free, and unreliable, while pattern matching is fast, has 100% recall on the
curated demo contract, and is exactly how real legal-tech tools operate. Each detected clause is
shown with severity, plain-English explanation, and rupee impact estimate over the contract
tenure.
Layer 5 — Hybrid Retaliation Detector
This is the layer that exists nowhere else in the market. The system tracks order volume per buyer
in a 90-day rolling window. When a legal action is taken (notice sent, complaint filed), the
detector compares pre-event and post-event order volume using a deterministic two-sigma test:
if the 30-day post-event mean drops more than 2σ below the 90-day pre-event mean, the system
flags retaliation with an explicit p-value. An Isolation Forest layer is added on top only for
buyers with more than 50 historical invoices — sparse-data anomaly detection produces too many
false positives to be trustworthy below that threshold.
When triggered, the system documents the pattern, generates a pre-filled MSME Ministry
complaint, and suggests buyer diversification based on the MSME’s industry cluster.
Layer 6 — Project Opaque (Privacy & Security Core)

The cryptographic foundation has three precisely-scoped components:
Fully Homomorphic Encryption (Zama concrete-ml) is applied strictly to the Buyer Reliability
scoring model. Numeric features (amounts, days, deltas, ratios) are encrypted client-side and the
XGBoost model runs inference on ciphertext server-side. The architectural purpose is to enable
future federated cloud aggregation without exposing individual MSME books — not to provide
local-only security, where FHE would add no benefit. This framing is honest, defensible, and
matches the technology’s actual capability.
AES-GCM symmetric encryption is applied to all string fields (buyer names, GSTINs, invoice
references, vendor names) that FHE cannot natively handle. This combination — FHE for
numeric ML inputs, AES-GCM for metadata — is the standard production pattern and must be
articulated explicitly in the demo.
Differential Privacy replaces the previously-considered ZK-SNARK layer, which is research-
grade and not implementable in 24 hours. When an MSME opts into the federated buyer
reputation network, Laplace noise (ε = 1.0, calibrated to invoice-amount sensitivity) is added to
defaulted invoice values before aggregation. This preserves the statistical signal across thousands
of submissions while making any individual transaction non-recoverable.
Encrypted USB Backup provides offline data sovereignty: a one-click export of the SQLite state
as an AES-256 encrypted archive. This is a five-minute build that produces an outsized trust
signal during demo Q&A.
Asynchronous FHE Caching addresses the 5–30 second inference latency: a Celery background
worker pre-computes FHE inferences and stores ciphertext results in a cache table. The Streamlit
UI polls only the cache, ensuring zero demo lag. When a new invoice is logged, the stale cache is
flagged and recomputed in background while the UI shows “Risk score updating…” rather than
freezing.
SHAP explainability runs client-side on the decrypted feature subset and a stored model-feature-
importance lookup, producing per-buyer waterfall charts that explain why a buyer’s reliability
score is what it is. This is critical for converting AI output from black-box to defensible.

§ 10 — Vulnerability Audit: Before vs After
The consolidation process explicitly resolved every vulnerability identified across prior plans. The
decryption-in-memory attack class is eliminated by FHE on numeric features paired with AES-
GCM on metadata. The previously over-claimed ZK-SNARK layer is replaced with deployable
Differential Privacy. The arbitrary 70% cold-start risk haircut is replaced with sector-baseline
lookups grounded in RBI data. The Gaussian variance assumption (μ + 1.5σ) is replaced with the
empirical 90th percentile, which respects the actual lognormal distribution of payment delays. The
sparse-data Isolation Forest false-positive risk is gated by the >50-invoice threshold. The Maker-
Checker race condition is closed by the separate pending_transactions table excluded from runway
math. The fragile time.sleep/st.rerun contagion animation is demoted to Tier 3 and reimplemented
as static pre-rendered HTML. The “Live FHE Terminal” hex-wall theater is replaced with a
structured panel showing inference latency. The legal-text hallucination risk is eliminated by f-
string templating with Llama 3 constrained to tone-paragraph only. The Section 43B(h) / Section
15 conflation is corrected throughout: 43B(h) is the Income Tax Act 1961 deduction-disallowance
rule; Section 15 is the MSMED Act 2006 45-day rule; both are cited in the correct contexts. The
“Hack us right now” demo framing is softened to a calibrated, defensible statement that doesn’t
invite questions about endpoint, session, or browser-localStorage attack vectors that FHE doesn’t
address. The fine-tuned BERT contract classifier is replaced with spaCy pattern matching, which
is faster, dataset-free, and gives 100% recall on the curated demo contract. The Twilio WhatsApp

dependency is replaced with Telegram (no business-account approval friction), with WhatsApp
listed as a production-roadmap item.

The cryptographic depth is genuine: FHE + AES-GCM + Differential Privacy is a coherent,
defensible, deployable privacy stack — not crypto theater. Five independent intelligence layers
(predictive ML, FHE privacy, contract NLP, retaliation detection, informal-cash bridge) tell one
integrated story that no single-feature submission can match. The demo emotion — Rohan
making payroll, the runway collapsing live, the retaliation alert firing — produces beats that
anomaly-detection dashboards cannot replicate. Every claim in this PRD is implementable in 24
hours by a 3–4 person team with the discipline to follow the Tier system. Every legal citation is
verified. Every cryptographic claim is correctly scoped. Every demo failure mode has a
documented mitigation.

The northstar: The MSME cash flow crisis is not a capital problem — it is a certainty problem.
Businesses can survive a 60-day payment delay if they know it’s coming. They cannot survive a 5-
day delay they didn’t see. InvoiceIQ sells certainty, manufactured from encrypted data that no one
— not even us — can read.
